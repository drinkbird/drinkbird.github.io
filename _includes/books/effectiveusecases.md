Use cases get a bad reputation when they collapse into either bullet lists nobody reads or UML diagrams nobody updates. Alistair Cockburn's book is the antidote: a practical guide to capturing behavior at the level that actually drives design decisions.

The core idea is "goal-oriented" writing. Each use case names a primary actor and the goal they are trying to accomplish, then walks the main success scenario and the extensions where things go wrong. The technique scales from a single paragraph to a fully dressed specification, and Cockburn is explicit about which level of detail is appropriate for which decisions.

What makes the book still relevant decades after it was written is the focus on goals over screens and on flows over data structures. Read it before your next requirements workshop and you will leave with sharper conversations, clearer acceptance criteria, and far fewer ambiguous tickets.
